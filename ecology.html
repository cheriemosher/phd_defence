Exploration in the Ecology of Larvae

I compared the gut-contents of larvae from stream reaches near the northern extent of the frog's range. I also related a set of environmental factors to the abundance of larvae and the spatial segregation of various developmental stages.













1. I used metabarcoding to compare the gut-content of larvae of A. truei from three different stream reaches and at two development stages.

Some DNA tells us apart, some DNA connects us.



I targeted a 157 base pair fragment at the 5' end of the cytochrome c oxidase subunit I (COI), as it is a efficient taxonomic identification tool.



In other words, I used the DNA in the guts of larva to put their food into taxonomic groups.



I focused on insects as potential prey.


















Count of Sequences


Table 1. Counts of COI sequences in the gut contents of larvae of A. truei.

















I created operational taxonomic units (OTUs) by grouping clusters with 97% similarity.



Compared OTUs to a database of sequences from known taxonomic classification.



Generated three datasets: all OTUs, OTUs with taxonomic assignment, OTUs within Arthropoda.


















Classify Groups of Closely Related Sequences


Table 2. Total sequences and operational taxonomic unit (OTU) counts of COI sequences in the gut contents of larvae of A. truei.

















Calculated alpha diversity, or average species diversity. This it the mean OTU count per sampled location or cohort age.



Calculated beta diversity, or differences in species composition. For beta diversity, 0 suggests complete overlap in taxonomic composition and 1 suggests no taxa in common.

Arthropods within Each Stream Reach


Table 3. Classifications to genus of OTUs within phylum Arthropoda by stream reach.


Seventy-two percent of OTU calls of the phylum Arthropoda were represented by a single sample within a sampled location, and 20% were represented by two samples.




















Differences in species diversity and species composition could be due to feeding preferences of individual larvae or the availability of different prey species among stream reaches.



The ‘Kalum Feeder’ eventually drains into Erlandsen Creek. Erlandsen Creek and Kalum Feeder had the lowest overlap in taxonomic composition.



My results suggest lotic invertebrates as likely prey.

























2. I quantified the relationship between an index of larval density and environmental factors measured within natal streams found at the northern extent of the species’ distribution.

Environmental Variables


Table 4. Variables used to model the influence of temporal, topographical, and habitat factors on the relative abundance and distribution of cohorts of the larvae of A. truei.





















I calculated the rate of larvae collected per hour as the number of larvae captured at a surveyed site divided by the total time at a site.



I used published classification schemes and personal observation to identify six cohort classes of larvae.




















Cohort Classifications


Table 5. Cohort categories and total captures per cohort for A. truei in northwestern British Columbia, Canada.





















I used negative binomial regression models to correlate the measured environmental variables with the larvae capture rate.



I used logistic regression models to correlate the measured environmental variables with the likelihood of capturing one cohort pool relative to another. I used multinomial model when > 2 pools.



Then used Akaike’s Information Criterion (AICc) for small sample sizes to select the most parsimonious model from the set of ecologically plausible models.

The capture rate of larvae decreased in a nonlinear fashion as stream width increased. The capture rate of larvae increased when stream depth exceeded 20 cm.



Slope was positively correlated with the presence of limbed larvae.
























A slow life history and reliance on fast-flowing streams suggest A. truei is sensitive to obstructions or modifications to streams.



My findings suggest that optimal habitat for larvae is related to stream width and depth.



Research will help inform where and how riparian management zones and habitat reserves are established, and the potential impacts of road and pipeline crossings along a stream reach.
