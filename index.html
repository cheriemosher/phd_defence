A. truei is an ancient species in the comparatively young habitat of the Coast Mountains in B.C.



They are of conservation interest because large portions of their range are managed for timber harvest.

















In the first part, I describe a phylogeography across the northern half of A. truei's range.



Phylogeography refers to the study of historical processes that influence contemporary geographic distributions.



This is crucial for developing effective conservation and management plans.

















In the second part, I describe an exploration into a few aspects of the ecology of larvae from stream reaches near the northern extent of the frog's range.



I compared the gut-contents of larvae from different stream reaches and at different development stages near the northern extent of the frog's range.



I also related a set of environmental factors to the abundance of larvae and the spatial segregation of various developmental stages.



Understanding specific habitat requirements helps identify key aspects of their environment to target for protection.
